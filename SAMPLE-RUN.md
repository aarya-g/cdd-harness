# Sample run: a $0 desk screen

This is an illustrative walkthrough on a **made-up product** ("PosturePing", a clip-on wearable that buzzes when you slouch, sold direct to remote workers). It is not a real candidate. It shows how the harness reasons, not a result to act on.

The engine runs every candidate through a 6-phase spine and closes each phase with a quantified, falsifiable claim before moving on. A candidate has to survive all six.

---

**Diagnose — is the problem real, acute, and funded?**
Slouching is real and widespread, but it is low-acuity: mild discomfort, not the kind of pain people open a wallet to stop today. Funded urgency is the gate, and this fails it.
Claim: fewer than 1 in 20 target buyers would call this a top-3 problem this month. Verdict so far: weak.

**Size — big enough to live on, small enough to own?**
The remote-worker TAM is huge, but the relevant sub-TAM is "people who will pay for a buzz reminder," which is small and poorly bounded. A big TAM with an undefined paying sub-TAM is a trap, not an opportunity.

**Price — what will the acute buyer actually pay?**
Willingness to pay is capped by a free substitute: phone and laptop apps already nudge posture at zero cost. Economic value versus the next-best alternative is thin, so price compresses toward zero.

**Position — what single slot do we own?**
The space is crowded (free apps plus an established wearable incumbent) and there is no defensible angle that a dedicated competitor could not copy in a month. No ownable slot.

**Capture — can we reach the buyer at zero ad spend?**
Posture and ergonomics content is saturated and dominated by high-authority health publishers. Ranking organically would be slow and expensive in effort. The zero-ad-spend path is not credible here.

**Measure — what would prove us wrong?**
Kill metric defined up front: if a one-week organic test cannot produce 25 qualified email signups at zero spend, the candidate is dead. We do not move money until that line is crossed.

---

**Verdict: DISCARD.**
Low acuity, a free substitute that caps price, and no ownable slot. The value of the engine is killing a plausible-looking product in an afternoon instead of finding out after the ad budget is gone. Most candidates end here, and that is the point.

> Honesty note: every claim above is directional and would be checked against real buyer evidence before any spend. Operators that depend on proprietary or unverified mechanics are tagged FLAG in the atlas and are never reconstructed.
